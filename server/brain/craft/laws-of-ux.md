# Laws of UX

Named cognitive principles that guide *what to compose* (vs the rendering-rule
files that guide *how to render*). Use them as a checklist when a layout's
structure hits a human limit.

## Choice and decisions
- **Hick's Law** — decision time grows with the number and complexity of
  options. Reduce and group choices; reveal advanced options progressively.
- **Choice overload** — too many equal options stalls action. On pricing, guide
  the eye to a recommended plan instead of presenting N identical cards.
- **Tesler's Law** — complexity is conserved; if it can't be removed, decide who
  absorbs it. Push it into sensible defaults, not onto the user.

## Attention and memory
- **Von Restorff (isolation)** — the item that differs is remembered. Make the
  primary action visually distinct; don't style every button the same.
- **Miller's Law / working memory** — people hold ~5–7 chunks at once. Chunk long
  forms, numbers, and nav into small groups.
- **Selective attention** — users ignore regions that look like ads or chrome.
  Keep key actions out of "banner-blind" zones.

## Motion and targets
- **Fitts's Law** — time to hit a target depends on its size and distance. Make
  primary and touch targets large (≥ 44px) and close to where the user already
  is. Put related controls near their object.
- **Doherty threshold** — keep system response under ~400ms (or show progress) to
  keep the user engaged and in flow.

## Progress and completion
- **Goal-gradient** — motivation rises near the finish. Show progress and make the
  remaining steps feel few (multi-step flows, onboarding).
- **Zeigarnik effect** — unfinished tasks nag; a visible checklist of what's left
  pulls users to complete it.
- **Peak–end rule** — people judge an experience by its most intense moment and
  its end. Invest in one delightful peak and a clean finish (success screen).

## Perception
- **Law of proximity / common region** — group related things by closeness and
  shared containers; separation implies "unrelated."
- **Jakob's Law** — users expect your product to work like the others they know.
  Honor established patterns; spend novelty budget only where it pays off.
- **Aesthetic–usability effect** — polished UI is perceived as more usable and
  forgiven more readily — but it does not excuse a broken flow.
